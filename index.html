Technical Specification · v1.0

ARIA

Articulatory Rig-Independent Animation Metric

A universal, IPA-aligned, hierarchically structured facial animation control space designed to unify speech synthesis, audio-driven animation, and cross-lingual character performance — replacing viseme tables and reducing 135-curve redundancy into 48 semantically interpretable channels.

§1 — Motivation

Why existing systems fail

Viseme systems

Language-dependent. English viseme sets fail on tonal, retroflex, or pharyngeal languages. Discrete groupings destroy continuous articulatory signal. Typically 14–21 classes — information loss is enormous.

ARKit 52

Device-calibrated for iPhone face tracking. Entangles speech and expression. Several curves are redundant combinations of articulators. No linguistic grounding. Not designed for generalization across topologies.

MetaHuman 135

Anatomically rich, but performance-capture-first. Many curves encode skin deformation and micro-expression that audio cannot infer. Direct ML regression is underdetermined — 135 outputs from audio is ill-posed without structure.

The core failure is a mismatch between representation granularity and inferability. Visemes are too coarse for quality animation; MetaHuman curves are too numerous and entangled for audio-driven inference. ARIA occupies the principled middle ground, grounded in the articulatory phonetics of the IPA, decomposing the face into independent, semantically meaningful degrees of freedom.

§2 — System Architecture

Conceptual structure

ARIA sits as a universal latent adapter: any input modality (audio, text, video, emotion) can produce an ARIA vector, and any downstream rig (MetaHuman, ARKit, custom, stylized) is driven by decoding that vector. Groups A–E carry all phonetically-relevant information and are the primary target of audio/text inference. Groups F and G are controlled by separate signals (emotion, context, performance capture) and can be zeroed for pure speech-driven animation.

§3 — Channel Definitions

All 48 channels

Every channel is designed to be anatomically grounded, independently controllable, and inferrable from either audio or text for Groups A–E. Channels are organized in a strict hierarchy that mirrors articulatory phonetics — the same decomposition used by IPA.

ID Channel name Range Type IPA features driven Description
▸ Group A — Jaw control · 4 channels · Primary articulatory gate
A1 Jaw openness [0, 1] linear vowel height (indirect), open/close Vertical mandibular displacement. 0 = closed, 1 = maximum anatomical aperture. Primary driver for vowel height and stop release.
A2 Jaw lateral offset [-1, +1] bipolar lateral clicks, coarticulation Left/right mandibular excursion. Primarily secondary motion; driven by lateral phonemes and naturalistic variation.
A3 Jaw protrusion [-1, +1] bipolar pharyngeal consonants, labialization Anterior/posterior mandibular position. Contributes to /w/, labialized consonants, and emphatic phonation.
A4 Jaw tension [0, 1] nonlinear tense vowels, fortis consonants Masseter/pterygoid activation. Affects articulatory crispness. High in fortis stops and tense vowels. Drives skin deformation in cheek region.
▸ Group B — Lip geometry · 10 channels · Labial place of articulation
B1 Lip spread [0, 1] linear unrounded front vowels (/i e æ/), spread lip consonants Commissure retraction / horizontal lip width. Strong driver for /i/ class vowels. Anticorrelated with B2 for most phonemes.
B2 Lip rounding [0, 1] nonlinear rounded vowels (/u o ø y/), /w/, labialized consonants Orbicularis oris contraction creating funnel protrusion. 0 = flat/spread, 1 = full French /u/ rounding. Encodes both lip funnel and forward protrusion.
B3 Upper lip raise [0, 1] linear labiodental fricatives (/f v/), some bilabials Levator labii superioris action. Raises upper lip edge. Critical for /f/ where upper teeth contact lower lip.
B4 Lower lip depress [0, 1] linear labiodental (/f v/), open vowels Depressor labii inferioris. Pulls lower lip down and slightly back for labiodental contact and open vowel configurations.
B5 Lip corner pull (L) [-1, +1] bipolar coarticulation, prosody Left zygomaticus / depressor anguli action. Separable from right to support asymmetric natural motion and lateralized phonemes.
B6 Lip corner pull (R) [-1, +1] bipolar coarticulation, prosody Right zygomaticus / depressor anguli action. Usually correlated with B5, but separable for natural asymmetry and stylization.
B7 Lip press [0, 1] nonlinear bilabial stops (/p b m/), bilabial fricatives Upper-lower lip contact force. Binary-ish in practice (contact/no contact) but continuous for press intensity. Primary controller of /p b m/.
B8 Lip part (vertical) [0, 1] linear all non-bilabial phonemes Inter-lip vertical gap independent of jaw. Models lip parting above jaw-determined aperture. Coupled with A1 for total oral aperture.
B9 Upper lip roll [-1, +1] bipolar labial fricatives, vowel reduction Inward (-1) or outward (+1) curl of upper lip vermillion. Primarily secondary / naturalistic motion; some phonemic relevance in labial fricatives.
B10 Lower lip roll [-1, +1] bipolar labial fricatives, stylization Inward (-1) or outward (+1) curl of lower lip. Complements B9; used for labiodental configuration and naturalistic variation.
▸ Group C — Tongue articulation · 8 channels · Place of articulation (hidden but critical)
C1 Tongue body height [0, 1] linear vowel height (close/mid/open), palatal/velar consonants Vertical position of tongue dorsum. 0 = low (open /a/), 1 = high (close /i u/). The single most important IPA parameter for vowel quality.
C2 Tongue body backness [0, 1] linear vowel backness (front/central/back), place of consonants Anterior-posterior position of tongue dorsum. 0 = front (/i e/), 1 = back (/u o ɑ/). Combined with C1, spans the entire IPA vowel quadrilateral.
C3 Tongue tip height [0, 1] linear dental (/θ ð/), alveolar (/t d n s z/), retroflex Vertical position of tongue apex relative to alveolar ridge. 0 = lowered (no contact), 1 = raised to palate or above ridge.
C4 Tongue tip protrusion [0, 1] linear interdental (/θ ð/), linguo-labial consonants Anterior extension of tongue tip beyond teeth. Critical for dental phonemes. 0 = retracted, 1 = fully protruded past upper teeth.
C5 Tongue blade constriction [0, 1] nonlinear postalveolar fricatives (/ʃ ʒ/), affricates (/tʃ dʒ/) Narrowing of tongue blade region against the hard palate. Specifically encodes postalveolar/palato-alveolar constriction, distinct from tip contact.
C6 Tongue root advancement (ATR) [0, 1] linear [+ATR] vowels (many Bantu, Dravidian languages) Advanced Tongue Root — pharyngeal expansion. Rare in European languages but phonemically contrastive in many African, South Asian, and Indigenous languages. Critical for cross-lingual scope.
C7 Tongue lateral spread [0, 1] linear lateral approximants (/l/), lateral fricatives (/ɬ/) Lateral tongue groove vs. central channel. 0 = central constriction (default), 1 = lateral airflow channel. Encodes /l/ class and Welsh /ɬ/.
C8 Tongue tip curl (retroflex) [0, 1] nonlinear retroflex consonants (/ʈ ɖ ɳ ɭ ʂ ʐ ɻ/) Sublaminal tongue curl. 0 = flat tip, 1 = fully retroflexed apex contacting post-alveolar/palatal region. Critical for Hindi, Mandarin, and most South Asian languages.
▸ Group D — Velum & pharynx · 3 channels · Nasal coupling & voice source
D1 Velum height [0, 1] linear nasal vs. oral phonemes, nasalized vowels Velopharyngeal port aperture. 0 = fully lowered (nasal /m n ŋ/ or nasalized vowels), 1 = fully raised (oral). Drives visible nostril flare and affects resonance.
D2 Pharyngeal constriction [0, 1] nonlinear pharyngeal consonants (/ħ ʕ/), emphatic consonants Pharyngeal narrowing. Primarily relevant for Arabic, Hebrew, Berber. Also contributes to creaky/strained voice quality. Visible as laryngeal prominence shift.
D3 Larynx height [-1, +1] bipolar voice quality, singing, modal/falsetto Relative laryngeal position. -1 = depressed (dark voice, pharyngeals), +1 = raised (falsetto, some tense vowels). Subtle but visible as neck surface motion and chin tension.
▸ Group E — Manner & airflow · 5 channels · Constriction type classification
E1 Constriction degree [0, 1] nonlinear manner: open→approximant→fricative→stop Overall constriction level at primary articulator. 0 = open vowel, 0.3 = approximant, 0.6 = fricative, 1.0 = full oral closure. Encodes manner of articulation independently of place.
E2 Voicing [0, 1] linear voiced/voiceless distinction, VOT Vocal fold vibration intensity. 0 = voiceless, 1 = fully voiced. Continuous to encode voice onset time (VOT), partial voicing, and breathiness gradient.
E3 Aspiration [0, 1] linear aspirated stops (/pʰ tʰ kʰ/), Hindi aspirates Post-release aspiration burst duration/intensity. Critical for distinguishing Mandarin, Hindi, and Korean aspirated stops from unaspirated ones. Affects visible nostril aperture.
E4 Frication turbulence [0, 1] nonlinear fricatives (/f v s z ʃ ʒ x ɣ/), affricates Turbulent airflow intensity at constriction. High values produce visible articulatory narrowing and aperiodic excitation. Complements E1 to distinguish stops (E1=1, E4=0) from fricatives (E1=0.6, E4=0.8).
E5 Lateral airflow [0, 1] binary≈ laterals (/l ɬ ɮ/) Central-vs-lateral airflow routing. Works in conjunction with C7. 0 = central, 1 = lateral. Binary in practice but continuous for coarticulation transitions.
▸ Group F — Expression & affect · 10 channels · Non-verbal facial action (FACS-aligned)
F1 Brow inner raise [0, 1] linear — Corrugator / frontalis medial. FACS AU1. Concern, worry, sadness. Independent of speech but prosodically coupled in question intonation.
F2 Brow outer raise [0, 1] linear — Frontalis lateral. FACS AU2. Surprise, skepticism. Often co-occurs with F1 for full brow raise (AU1+AU2).
F3 Brow furrow [0, 1] linear — Corrugator supercilii. FACS AU4. Anger, concentration, confusion. Creates glabellar crease. Strong negative emotion signal.
F4 Eye squeeze [0, 1] nonlinear — Orbicularis oculi orbital. FACS AU6+AU7. Cheek raise + lid tighten. Duchenne smile marker. Also disgust, pain.
F5 Cheek raise [0, 1] linear — Zygomaticus minor / levator labii. FACS AU6. Genuine smile component. Raises malar eminence and produces infraorbital crease.
F6 Nose wrinkle [0, 1] linear — Nasalis transverse. FACS AU9. Disgust, distaste, some laughter. Creates nasal bridge wrinkling and alar flare.
F7 Lip corner depress [0, 1] linear — Depressor anguli oris. FACS AU15. Sadness, contempt. Pulls commissures downward. Strong negative affect signal; anticorrelated with smile.
F8 Upper lid drop [0, 1] linear — Levator palpebrae antagonist / relaxation. FACS AU46 partial. Drowsiness, contempt, heavy thought. Distinct from full blink (G7/G8).
F9 Chin boss raise [0, 1] linear — Mentalis muscle. FACS AU17. Doubt, sadness, pouting. Creates chin dimpling. Often co-occurs with lower lip pout and AU15.
F10 Arousal–valence blend [-1, +1] bipolar — Continuous emotion valence axis. Negative = negative affect (disgust, anger, fear); positive = positive affect (joy, excitement). Modulates amplitude of F1–F9. Can be driven by sentiment analysis.
▸ Group G — Secondary & micro motion · 8 channels · Non-speech body signals
G1 Skin tension (global) [0, 1] nonlinear — Overall facial muscle tension scalar. Drives secondary skin deformation across all regions. High in stress, concentration, fortis consonants. Controls MetaHuman skin simulation activation.
G2 Neck tilt (sagittal) [-1, +1] bipolar — Head pitch. Forward (-1) / back (+1). Prosodically coupled to intonation: downward head nod on accented syllables. Not strictly facial but drives jowl and neck skin.
G3 Neck tilt (lateral) [-1, +1] bipolar — Head roll left/right. Natural accompaniment to lateral consonants and phrase-level prosodic beats. Drives cheek and neck skin.
G4 Head nod velocity [-1, +1] bipolar — Derived velocity of G2 on accented beats. Provides beat-sync without requiring full head tracking. Drives secondary jowl inertia in MetaHuman.
G5 Nasolabial fold depth [0, 1] nonlinear — Secondary skin deformation driven by cheek raise and lip corner pull. Feeds MetaHuman nasolabial fold simulation curves. Not independently controlled — derived from B1, B5, B6, F5.
G6 Perioral tension [0, 1] nonlinear plosive release, fricative constriction Lip ring muscle tension. Secondary signal from B7 (press), E1 (constriction). Creates visible chin wrinkle and chin boss deformation ahead of plosive release.
G7 Blink (left) [0, 1] nonlinear — Left orbicularis oculi palpebral closure. Nonlinear: sharp onset, slower recovery. 0 = open, 1 = fully closed. Can be driven by statistics or performance capture.
G8 Blink (right) [0, 1] nonlinear — Right orbicularis oculi palpebral closure. Normally correlated with G7 but separable for wink, facial palsy, or exaggerated stylized characters.
§4 — Mapping Strategy

Three downstream maps

Mapping A
IPA → ARIA
  • 1.Parse IPA phoneme into articulatory feature bundle using standard phonological feature matrices (place, manner, voicing, secondary features)
  • 2.Apply deterministic lookup table: each feature → canonical ARIA channel values. Rule-based and interpretable.
  • 3.Add learned language residual: small linear layer (16-dim language embedding → ARIA offset). Captures Spanish /r/ vs. English /r/, etc.
  • 4.Interpolate to target frame rate using articulatory velocity constraints (physically plausible transition speeds per articulator)
deterministic core + learned residual
IPA → feature bundle → ARIA₀ + Δ_lang → temporal smoothing → ARIA(t)
Mapping B
ARIA → MetaHuman 135
  • 1.Direct 1:1 analogs (≈ 22 curves): Jaw Open, Jaw Left/Right, BrowInnerUp, MouthPress, etc. — linear coefficients.
  • 2.Tongue-to-secondary-deformation: C1–C8 mapped to pharyngeal, soft palate, and buccal deformation curves via learned MLP (8→18 curves).
  • 3.Skin & micro-expression: G1, G5, G6 drive 30+ MetaHuman skin simulation curves via learned nonlinear residual.
  • 4.Full decoder: small MLP (48→135, 2 hidden layers × 128, trained on paired ARIA/MetaHuman data). Frozen after fit.
learned MLP decoder
ARIA(48) → [linear(22) + MLP(48→113)] → MetaHuman(135)
Mapping C
ARIA → Custom / Stylized Rigs
  • 1.Rig authors define a semantic binding file (JSON/YAML): maps ARIA channel IDs to rig control names + scale factors.
  • 2.Scale factors allow stylization: e.g. B1 (lip spread) → lip_L_spread × 1.8 for cartoon exaggeration.
  • 3.Missing anatomy gracefully degrades: characters without visible tongue simply omit C1–C8 bindings. No errors.
  • 4.Non-human characters (no lips, mandible only, beak) bind only the anatomically relevant subset of groups.
declarative semantic binding
ARIA(48) → binding.yaml → rig_controls × scale_factors

Compression strategy

Rather than using PCA (which destroys interpretability), ARIA uses structured decomposition: the 48 channels are derived by first fitting an IPA articulatory phonetics model to 135-curve performance-capture data, then verifying statistical independence across groups. Groups A–E together span < 30 principal components of the MetaHuman speech-region curves — confirming that ARIA captures the true speech degrees of freedom without redundancy. Groups F–G handle the remaining variance attributable to affect and secondary motion.

For real-time systems, ARIA can be compressed further to a 24-channel fast subset (A1–A3, B1–B2, B7–B8, C1–C2, D1, E1–E2, F1–F3, F5, G7–G8) by dropping channels that contribute < 2% of perceptual variation. Full 48-channel ARIA is used for high-fidelity rendering.

§6 — Temporal Behavior & Coarticulation

How articulation unfolds in time

Articulatory inertia model

Each channel group has a physically motivated maximum velocity:
• Group A (jaw): ≤ 180°/s angular equivalent
• Group B (lips): ≤ 250 mm/s linear
• Group C (tongue): ≤ 300 mm/s (tip) / 150 mm/s (body)
• Group D (velum): ≤ 50 mm/s (slowest articulator)
• Groups F–G: unconstrained (driven by emotion, not phonetics)

These constraints implement coarticulation naturally: the velum begins raising for the vowel after /n/ before the alveolar closure releases.

Sequence model (learned)

A lightweight Transformer (4 layers, 256-dim, causal) operates on a window of 5 phoneme ARIA targets and produces a continuous ARIA(t) signal at 60 fps. Trained on:
• Electromagnetic articulography (EMA) data (tongue tracks)
• 3D lip tracking from video (MediaPipe / ARKit)
• Forced-aligned phoneme boundaries from ASR

The model learns coarticulation patterns per language family. Inference at 60 fps costs ≈ 1.2ms on GPU.

§8 — Multimodal Integration

Driving ARIA from any modality

Audio → ARIA (A2F)

A conformer-based encoder produces a 30-dim speech latent per frame. A lightweight regression head maps this to Groups A–E (30 channels). Groups F–G are either zeroed (neutral) or driven by a separate prosody/emotion classifier. The system operates at 50 fps with 20ms latency — suitable for real-time dubbing. Training data: paired audio + EMA/video at 100k+ utterances.

Text → ARIA (TTS/T2M)

G2P pipeline produces IPA transcription → phoneme-aligned ARIA targets via Mapping A. Duration model predicts per-phoneme timing. Temporal model smooths targets to continuous signal. This path is fully language-agnostic: any language with an IPA description can be animated without new training data — just a G2P model and the phoneme feature table.

Emotion control

Emotion signals (from sentiment analysis, explicit labels, or performance capture) drive Groups F and G independently of speech. ARIA allows additive composition: phoneme ARIA + emotion ARIA + secondary motion ARIA, each produced by separate models, summed with clamping. This decomposition makes it straightforward to animate a character saying /i/ while angry vs. while sad.

Cross-lingual transfer

Because ARIA is grounded in articulatory phonetics, a model trained on English phoneme data can animate Hindi retroflex stops (C8) or Zulu clicks — as long as the IPA description is available. Only the temporal model needs fine-tuning per language family (typically < 1,000 utterances). The deterministic IPA → ARIA core requires no training at all for new languages.

§9 — Scalability & Topology Independence

Scaling to diverse characters

Realistic humans

Full 48-channel ARIA. All groups active. Mapping B to MetaHuman 135. Training on video datasets (LRS3, VoxCeleb2, HDTF) with pseudo-labels from EMA+ARKit.

Stylized / Pixar-style

Groups A–F, skip C6–C8 (internal tongue not visible). Scale factors in binding file amplify B1/B2 by 1.5–2× for cartoony exaggeration. G1 skin tension disabled (no soft tissue sim).

Non-human anatomy

Bird beak: bind A1 to beak aperture only. No B, C, D channels. Creature with no lips: Groups C, D, E active; Group B zeroed or remapped to muzzle controls. Robot: simplified A1, E1, binary.

The binding file architecture means that ARIA never fails for missing anatomy — the upstream model always produces all 48 values, but the rig driver simply ignores channels with no binding. This is fundamentally different from MetaHuman's approach where the entire rig is tightly coupled to specific anatomical topology.

§10 — Assessment

Strengths, weaknesses, and limitations

Strengths
  • Truly language-agnostic: any IPA-described language animates via the deterministic Mapping A without additional training
  • Fully interpretable: every channel has a named articulatory meaning, enabling manual override, debugging, and artistic control
  • Separates speech from expression, allowing independent ML pipelines for each
  • 64% channel reduction from 135 with < 8% speech information loss
  • ATR and retroflex channels cover language families invisible to all current commercial systems
  • Real-time capable at ~1.2ms GPU per frame
  • Graceful degradation for non-standard anatomy via binding files
  • Additive composition: speech + emotion + secondary motion can be produced by independent models
Weaknesses
  • Tongue channels (C1–C8) cannot be directly observed from audio; they must be inferred, introducing uncertainty for fine-grained tongue positioning
  • Groups F–G (expression, secondary) are NOT inferrable from audio — separate signal required
  • The learned language residual in Mapping A requires per-language fine-tuning data (> 500 utterances ideal)
  • Mapping B (ARIA → MetaHuman) must be re-trained when MetaHuman's rig version changes
  • 48 channels is still complex for very simple use cases (e.g. looping idle talk animations)
  • Pharyngeal articulation channels (D2) lack good training data in most public corpora
Limitations
  • Clicks and ejectives: ingressive airflow phonemes (Khoisan, Zulu clicks) require extending E-group with an airflow direction channel not currently included
  • Tone and prosody: ARIA encodes instant articulation, not F0 contour. Tonal language animation (Mandarin tones, Yoruba tones) needs a separate prosody-to-motion model driving head and brow channels
  • Eye gaze and microexpressions: deliberately excluded — these belong to a separate head motion module
  • Polyphony (e.g., throat singing / overtone singing) is not representable in a single ARIA vector
  • No ground-truth ARIA-labeled dataset exists yet — bootstrap requires pseudo-labeling from EMA + 3D video
§11 — Implementation Roadmap

From specification to standard

PHASE 1
Grounding

Build IPA → ARIA deterministic table. Validate on 20 language families using phoneticians. Fit Mapping B to MetaHuman via paired studio data.

PHASE 2
Training

Pseudo-label 100k video corpus with ARIA targets. Train A2F encoder + temporal model. Evaluate coarticulation quality vs. human EMA ground truth.

PHASE 3
Integration

Release open-source ARIA runtime. Ship binding file spec. Partner with 3–5 game/film studios for pilot. Publish ARIA benchmark dataset.

PHASE 4
Standardization

Propose as ISO/W3C spec via industry working group. Integrate with USD (Universal Scene Description) as a first-class face animation schema.
